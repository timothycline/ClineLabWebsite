---
title: "Streamflow & Fish Production"
description: "How climate-driven drought and water demand shape the productivity and resilience of freshwater fish populations across the northern Rockies."
weight: 1
---

Understanding how environmental conditions regulate fish production is foundational to both ecological theory and the sustainable management of freshwater ecosystems. As climate change accelerates hydrological variability and human demands on water grow, it is increasingly urgent to identify the mechanisms by which streamflow and water availability regulate recruitment, survival, and carrying capacity in riverine fish populations.

This is the central focus of our research program. We integrate long-term demographic datasets, field studies, and quantitative modeling to uncover how drought and flow variability shape fish production across the northern Rocky Mountains. A flagship synthesis of this work — examining how streamflow drought drives fish production and ecosystem capacity — is currently in review at *Nature* (Cline et al., in review). This research has been developed in close partnership with Montana Fish, Wildlife & Parks, NOAA, and the U.S. Geological Survey, and is the foundation for building real-time drought early warning systems for fisheries and water managers.

We are also pursuing more fundamental questions about how energy availability shapes fish production. While climate refugia strategies for cold-water species typically focus on thermal habitat, our work examines how gross primary production (GPP) — the base of the aquatic food web — interacts with temperature to determine where fish actually thrive. MS student **Benny Bevil** is leading research that integrates metabolic theory with watershed-scale GPP estimates to evaluate which streams support high fish production, aiming to inform the next generation of habitat conservation planning that prioritizes productive watersheds, not just cold ones.

This theme is also the basis for a collaborative NSF proposal in development with Drs. Wyatt Cross (MSU), Tim Covino (MSU), Robert Hall (University of Montana), and Anna Marshall (University of Tennessee) to study how flow regimes regulate energy transfer to higher trophic levels across river networks.
