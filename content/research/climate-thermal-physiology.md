---
title: "Climate Change & Thermal Physiology"
description: "How rising water temperatures affect fish growth, survival, and reproduction — and what that means for populations under future climate scenarios."
weight: 1
---

Cold-water fishes like cutthroat trout, bull trout, and Chinook salmon are among the most thermally sensitive vertebrates on the continent. As stream temperatures rise with climate change, these species face direct physiological stress and indirect effects through altered food webs, disease dynamics, and competitive interactions with warm-water species.

We measure thermal performance across a range of biological scales — from enzyme kinetics and metabolic rate to individual growth and population-level persistence — and integrate those measurements with downscaled climate projections and stream temperature models. The goal is to identify which populations are most vulnerable, where habitat refugia exist, and how management interventions can buy time for adaptation.

**Current directions include:**

- Measuring critical thermal maxima (CTmax) and aerobic scope across genetically distinct trout populations
- Building spatially explicit vulnerability models for Rocky Mountain trout under RCP 4.5 and 8.5 scenarios
- Examining whether local thermal adaptation can offset climate-driven range contractions
- Partnering with state agencies to embed vulnerability assessments into habitat restoration prioritization
