---
title: "Life-History Diversity & Resilience"
description: "How variation in life-history strategies — when, where, and how individuals grow and reproduce — buffers fish populations against environmental change and biological invasions."
weight: 2
---

Life-history diversity — the variation in how, when, and where individuals grow, survive, and reproduce — is a key mechanism by which fish populations maintain productivity and buffer against environmental change. A population composed of individuals with diverse strategies is less likely to collapse when conditions shift, because some portion of the population will always be well-suited to prevailing conditions. Understanding the causes and consequences of this diversity is central to both ecological theory and the management of freshwater species facing rapid environmental and biological change.

Our lab investigates life-history diversity across several complementary projects, united by the use of otolith microchemistry and stable isotopes to reconstruct individual life histories in ways that traditional surveys cannot capture.

**Native trout recruitment.** PhD student **Nick Hudson** is studying life-history variation in native trout across southwest Montana, combining otolith microchemistry, stable isotope analysis, and mark-recapture data to determine where trout are produced and how they move through habitats across their life cycle. This work, funded by Montana Fish, Wildlife & Parks, aims to identify the specific habitats critical to each life stage and inform recruitment-focused management strategies.

**Invasive rainbow trout.** MS student **Lukas Draugelis** is examining the invasion dynamics of rainbow trout in the Flathead River system — specifically how life-history strategies evolve and diversify along an active invasion front. His work addresses how phenotypic plasticity and rapid adaptation facilitate spread, directly informing FWP's ongoing suppression efforts.

**Bull trout and lake trout invasion.** Postdoctoral researcher **Madaline Cochrane** is testing how life-history diversity in bull trout has been altered by the invasion of non-native lake trout in the Flathead Basin. This work builds on a growing collaboration with graduate students at the University of Washington and University of Utah, and has produced multiple papers using otolith microchemistry to understand how populations respond to environmental pressures and biological invasions (Brennan et al. 2024, Mahklouf et al. 2025).
