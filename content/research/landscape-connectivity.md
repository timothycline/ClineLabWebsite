---
title: "Landscape Connectivity"
description: "Identifying which habitat features limit fish movement and gene flow among fragmented populations, and where restoration will have the greatest impact."
weight: 3
---

Stream networks in the American West have been fragmented by thousands of dams, road-stream crossings, and diversions. For cold-water fish that depend on access to spawning and rearing habitats distributed across entire watersheds, these barriers can be as consequential as habitat loss itself.

We apply landscape genetics, circuit theory, and movement ecology to map connectivity across fragmented networks and identify the bottlenecks that most limit population exchange. This work directly informs barrier prioritization — helping agencies decide which culverts to replace, which dams to remove, and where reintroduction efforts are most likely to succeed.

**Current directions include:**

- RADseq-based landscape genomics of bull trout across tributary networks in western Montana
- Developing resistance surfaces that integrate stream temperature, passage barriers, and flow intermittency
- Evaluating post-restoration gene flow recovery after culvert replacements
- Collaborating with Tribes and agencies on anadromous fish reintroduction feasibility assessments
