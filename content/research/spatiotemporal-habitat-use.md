---
title: "Spatiotemporal Habitat Use"
description: "How density, habitat structure, and evolutionary trade-offs shape where fish go — and what that means for populations, ecosystems, and the people who depend on them."
weight: 4
---

Understanding how animals select and use habitat across space and time is central to ecology, evolution, and conservation. Habitat use drives population dynamics, gene flow, species interactions, and the effectiveness of management interventions. Yet the mechanisms and consequences of habitat selection remain understudied, particularly under scenarios of crowding, habitat degradation, and rapid environmental change.

This is an emerging and theoretically ambitious direction in our research program. Recent work with sockeye salmon spawning distributions has provided new insight into how population density interacts with habitat structure to shape spatial aggregation. We developed and applied a novel use of the Chinese Restaurant Process — a model from Bayesian nonparametrics — to describe how salmon distribute themselves across stream habitats and how this varies with density and geomorphic constraints (Cline and Schindler, submitted to *Journal of Animal Ecology*). This work revealed key differences in habitat selection strategies across streams and raises broader questions about the evolutionary trade-offs driving aggregation versus dispersion under crowding.

Building on this, we are developing ideas with **Ryan Kovach** (Montana Fish, Wildlife & Parks) that explore the eco-evolutionary consequences of density-dependent habitat selection. Our work challenges classic assumptions about how density dependence contributes to natural selection — particularly the distinction between "hard" and "soft" selection regimes — and aims to test how these forms of selection structure trait distributions and population outcomes in spatially heterogeneous environments.

We are also extending these concepts to human spatial behavior. In collaboration with PhD student **Michael Lant**, we are applying ecological theory to understand how anglers distribute themselves across landscapes in ways that parallel animal habitat selection. This synthesis of behavioral ecology and human dimensions explores how crowding, perceived habitat quality, and social information shape recreational spatial dynamics — providing tools for fisheries planning while expanding the reach of habitat selection theory into applied social-ecological contexts.
