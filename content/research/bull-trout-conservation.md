---
title: "Bull Trout Conservation Science"
description: "Generating actionable science on population dynamics, threats, and recovery strategies for a federally threatened species across the Pacific Northwest and northern Rocky Mountains."
weight: 3
---

Bull trout are a native char and a central conservation concern throughout the Pacific Northwest and northern Rocky Mountains. Listed as threatened under the Endangered Species Act, populations have declined due to habitat fragmentation, non-native species, altered flow regimes, and climate change. Despite their protected status, major knowledge gaps remain regarding population productivity, connectivity, and recovery potential. Our lab is addressing these gaps through close collaboration with state, federal, and tribal agencies, producing science that is directly applicable to recovery planning, monitoring strategies, and policy.

**Population dynamics and recovery benchmarks.** Postdoctoral researcher **Madaline Cochrane** has led work on bull trout in the Kootenai River basin, using long-term data to quantify drivers of productivity in Lake Koocanusa and assess potential for a sustainable recreational fishery (*Ecological Applications*, in press). She is now building an integrated population model to understand how operations at Libby Dam have influenced demographic connectivity and long-term population viability. In parallel, we developed stock-recruitment models with collaborators at the USGS to establish science-based recovery benchmarks — tools that help managers assess the demographic health of populations across recovery units (Glassic et al., in review, *Canadian Journal of Fisheries and Aquatic Sciences*).

**Translocation as a recovery tool.** MS student **Ben Weber** is conducting one of the first rigorous field evaluations of bull trout translocation, assessing both the fate of translocated individuals and the broader ecological effects of introducing a threatened top predator into novel ecosystems. This work, funded by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, directly addresses a management strategy that agencies are increasingly considering as wild populations continue to decline.

**Transboundary and flow-dependent populations.** We led a population assessment for the Milk River to inform instream flow targets under the international water compact — defining the flow conditions necessary to sustain bull trout populations on both sides of the Canadian border under a changing climate.

**Agency engagement.** PI Tim Cline serves as a steering committee member of the Bull Trout Interagency Recovery Team (BIRT), where he provides scientific guidance on recovery planning, monitoring, and policy across the Upper Columbia recovery unit.
