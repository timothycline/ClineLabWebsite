Invasive rainbow trout pose a major threat to native bull trout and westslope cutthroat trout in the Flathead River system. Despite ongoing suppression efforts, the mechanisms that allow rainbow trout to successfully invade and establish remain poorly understood, particularly the role of life-history plasticity in facilitating spread.

MS student **Lukas Draugelis** is examining how life-history strategies — age at maturation, growth rates, habitat use, migratory behavior — vary across the invasion front and how this variation may enable or constrain further expansion. By combining otolith analysis, genetic sampling, and telemetry, this work will reveal how rapidly evolving life-history traits contribute to invasion success.

Findings will directly inform FWP's suppression efforts by identifying which life-history phenotypes are most responsible for recruitment and spread, and which habitat conditions or population stages represent the best targets for intervention.
