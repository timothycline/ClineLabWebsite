As wild bull trout populations continue to decline across much of their range, translocation — moving fish from healthy donor populations to augment or establish populations elsewhere — is increasingly being considered by managers. Yet rigorous evaluations of translocation success are virtually absent from the bull trout literature.

MS student **Ben Weber** is conducting one of the first field-based assessments of bull trout translocation efficacy, tracking the survival, movement, and reproductive output of translocated individuals using telemetry and genetic sampling. The project also evaluates the broader ecological effects of reintroducing a threatened apex predator into systems from which it has been absent, including effects on prey fish communities and ecosystem structure.

Results will provide the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service with critical information to evaluate whether and when translocation should be incorporated into recovery planning, and to identify the donor and recipient population characteristics that predict success.
