Wild trout populations in southwest Montana's rivers — including the Big Hole, Madison, and Beaverhead — support some of the most valuable recreational fisheries in the state, yet the ecological drivers of recruitment variability remain poorly understood. This project combines mark-recapture, otolith microchemistry, stable isotope analysis, and habitat surveys to determine where trout are produced, how they move through river networks across their life cycle, and which habitats are most critical for sustaining wild populations.

PhD student **Nick Hudson** is leading this work, with close collaboration from regional Montana FWP fisheries biologists. The project is designed to produce recruitment-focused management recommendations that target habitat investment toward life stages and locations where it will have the greatest demographic impact.

**Key questions:**
- What habitats produce the most recruits, and what environmental conditions regulate annual variation in recruitment?
- How do individual life-history strategies — residency vs. migration, early vs. late spawning — vary across populations and years?
- Which reaches function as population sources vs. sinks, and how does this change with flow and temperature?
