The Upper Clark Fork River downstream of Anaconda carries one of the largest Superfund legacies in the United States, with heavy metals contamination from historic copper smelting affecting water quality, stream substrate, and aquatic food webs across hundreds of miles of river. Despite ongoing Superfund remediation, wild trout populations in many reaches remain depressed relative to seemingly suitable habitat — suggesting that legacy stressors continue to impose demographic bottlenecks that are not fully understood.

This project, which will support a new MS student, examines the role of heavy metals in shaping juvenile trout survival and movement in the Clark Fork. By combining intensive mark-recapture sampling, telemetry, and water and tissue chemistry, we aim to quantify how contamination gradients interact with flow variability, temperature, and habitat availability to drive mortality at critical early life stages.

**Key questions:**
- Where and when does heavy metals exposure cause measurable mortality in juvenile brown trout?
- How do contamination effects interact with drought and flow variability to constrain population recovery?
- Which reaches represent the greatest bottlenecks to population recovery, and how do those priorities align with ongoing Superfund remediation efforts?

Results will be developed in close collaboration with Montana Fish, Wildlife & Parks and are intended to inform both remediation priorities and trout population management in the Clark Fork drainage.
