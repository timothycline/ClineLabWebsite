Drought is one of the most acute and growing threats to freshwater fish populations in the American West, yet managers currently lack the tools to anticipate drought impacts in time to act. This project addresses that gap by developing empirical models that link streamflow conditions to fish population responses, and translating those models into decision-support tools accessible to fisheries managers and water resource agencies.

Our approach integrates long-term fish population monitoring data from across the northern Rocky Mountains with hydrological records, drought indices, and climate projections. A flagship synthesis of this work — examining how streamflow drought drives fish production and carrying capacity in river ecosystems — has been submitted to *Nature* (Cline et al., in review).

This project is being carried out in close collaboration with Montana Fish, Wildlife & Parks, NOAA, and the U.S. Geological Survey, and supports two full-time postdoctoral researchers in the Cline Lab.

**Key outputs:**
- Empirical models linking drought severity to fish recruitment and population-level production
- Real-time drought risk dashboards for fisheries managers
- A synthesis manuscript submitted to *Nature*
